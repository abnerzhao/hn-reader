## easy

AI can answer questions and make decisions very quickly. This is useful, but it can also make us stop thinking for ourselves. I keep asking where help from AI ends and dependence begins.

Some tasks are good to give to AI, such as translation, routine research, or repetitive work. But important questions need our own ideas first. When we let AI choose everything for us, we may lose practice in making choices and forming opinions.

I try to think before I ask. Make guesses, discuss ideas, and then use AI to test or add to your thinking. AI should help us learn and decide, not replace our judgment.

## medium

AI makes it easy to hand over more of our thinking. It can research, reason, and give a finished answer in minutes. That convenience can be useful, but it also removes the intermediate work of breaking down a question, comparing sources, and forming an answer.

At a startup event, my friend met a man who records every conversation and lets an AI system summarize and analyze them all. He believes the system is better at critical thinking than he is. This raises a broader question: who makes the final decisions that matter in our lives?

There is value in slow thinking. On phone-free walks, questions come to my mind that I would normally search immediately. Most are forgotten, while the few important ones remain. Not every thought needs an instant answer.

While travelling in Portugal, my sister and I discussed colonial history before asking AI. We offered theories, disagreed, remembered details, and accepted that some guesses could be wrong. Only after that did we use AI to test and extend our ideas.

AI can make work and learning more efficient. It can translate reports, support research, and help students study. Yet it can also produce polished answers without teaching someone how to solve a problem. The same tool can support learning or replace it.

The goal is not complete independence from AI. It is to keep human agency in the process: choosing the questions, providing judgment, and deciding what matters. We should ask whether we are automating routine tasks or our ability to think and choose.

## hard

I have been observing, in myself and in those around me, a tendency to increasingly offload our thinking to AI. From trivial decisions to complex thinking, it is easy, convenient, and in some cases, encouraged, to use AI for researching, reasoning, and answering our every query.

I recently read “The Perfect Match” by Ken Liu, a 2012 short story which describes this phenomenon with unexpected accuracy. In the story, a universal AI assistant named Tilly serves users by offering useful and enjoyable recommendations. The main character asks Tilly what he should do for breakfast and asks it to find him a suitable person to date. He does not know what he wants to eat, what music he would like to hear, or what to say on his date. “Who knows your tastes and moods better than I?” Tilly asks.

My friend recently went to a San Francisco startup event, where he met a man with a small microphone pinned to his shirt. The man recorded all of his conversations and later used a workflow to summarize and analyze them. He said, “I think Claude Fable is smarter than me. It’s better at critical thinking than I am, so I let Fable do all of my thinking these days.”

Before Claude, ChatGPT, and Gemini became household names, we were already offloading parts of our thinking to search engines. But search still required us to break down a question, evaluate sources, and synthesize an answer. AI increasingly performs those intermediate steps for us, producing a finished response to even complex questions in minutes. It saves you time, and it saves you thinking.

There is a fine line between having an assistant that helps with your tasks and losing all of your autonomy. Perhaps the question to ask is: who is making the final decisions for the things that really matter to you in your life?

The offloading of thinking to AI creeps into my life, too. There will always be some tradeoff between slow thinking and quick answers. Many questions merit quick answers, but many others merit longer thinking.

Sometimes, I walk around my neighborhood without my phone. Questions pop into my head that I am used to looking up immediately. By the time I get home, I forget most of them. I remember the important few, and assume the rest were insignificant enough to forget. Maybe there is some value in forgetting the trivial, in not having an immediate answer to every query that appears in our minds.

A few months ago, I was travelling in Portugal with my sister. We wondered why Portugal seemed proud of its colonial history and why its response to colonialism seemed different from the way the United States talks about its own history. My sister said, “Let’s ask ChatGPT.”

I suggested that we pause and think about why this might be. We offered theories, made wild guesses, backtracked, connected ideas, disagreed, and remembered historical details from high school. We knew we were speculating, and some theories were probably wrong. That was part of the exercise.

Eventually, we asked the same question to AI. Its response corroborated many of our theories and supplied explanations we had missed. It also omitted possibilities we still found plausible. We had begun with a question, generated hypotheses, and only then used AI to test and extend our thinking. I relished the exercise.

I work in AI, measuring Gemini’s capabilities in solving hard tasks. I also see people describe how AI has helped them at work. It can translate long reports, support research, help coding agents implement details, and act as a personalized tutor. If AI automates routine, repetitive, and tedious tasks, it may free people to do more rewarding thinking.

But the ease of using AI can also lead to lazy thinking. My mother teaches physics at an online university and suspects many students complete assignments with AI. Some responses are nearly identical, as if students copied the question into the same tool without a single original thought. The answers may be comprehensive, but what is the point of school if students never learn how to arrive at them?

AI can support learning, but it can also produce an answer without teaching you how to reach it. Solving a physics problem or writing an essay may feel tedious, but the process is part of learning.

There is no clear way to separate full autonomy of thinking from automating menial work. It is often some blend. What matters may be whether we still collect and curate the data, formulate the questions, and evaluate the results.

In Ken Liu’s story, Jenny objects to the main character’s reliance on Tilly: “Tilly doesn’t just tell you what you want! She tells you what to think. Do you even know what you really want anymore?” Our autonomy depends, at least in part, on continuing to participate in forming our own desires.

What are we automating? Human work or human agency? Human tasks or human thinking?
