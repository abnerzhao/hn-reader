## easy

When I was young, I loved reading. Books were everywhere in my room. They showed me new worlds and gave me time to think.

Later, reading became hard. School, work, friends, and my phone took my attention. I checked my phone again and again, hoping for a new message. A book felt quiet and slow.

Now I use small rules to read again. I turn off notifications and put my phone in airplane mode. Then I read for thirty minutes. It is not easy, but books help me feel calm and think deeply.

## medium

I reached my reading peak when I was eleven or twelve. My bedside table was covered with books at different stages of being read, and new piles appeared on the floor and in other rooms. Books felt like knowledge, a window into many worlds, and perhaps a key to adulthood.

That appetite did not last. As I grew older, reading seemed socially inconvenient. School required its own reading, friends and entertainment competed for my attention, and later work and relationships made a quiet hour with a book feel almost selfish.

The strongest opponent was the phone. Even when I was not posting much online, I was still checking for mail, news, and notifications. Each glance offered the small possibility of good news. A serious book could not promise anything immediate in return.

Eventually I noticed what this had done to me. I had become restless when I was alone, and my inner life felt thinner. Reading had once been an invitation into another person’s world; scrolling mostly kept me inside a noisy flow of updates.

My recovery has been ordinary and slightly embarrassing. I turn off notifications and use airplane mode for half an hour at a time. The goal is simply to protect the ability to pay attention.

## hard

I hit my reading peak when I was eleven or twelve. My bedside table was stacked with books, all at different stages of being read; books migrated to the floor beside the bed, and then to different rooms of the apartment. I was ravenous. Books seemed to be essentially the same as knowledge, a window into all kinds of worlds and, crucially, a key into adulthood.

Since then my reading has been in decline and under siege from a variety of adversaries. Adolescence made it seem socially catastrophic; school turned reading into an obligation; social life and work suggested that opening a book for pleasure was an admission that I had failed to join the flow of ordinary life.

But the most significant obstacle was social media. The regular checking of a phone is like a slot machine: each glance carries the possibility that something might be waiting for you, an invitation, an opportunity, a compliment. Nothing else can easily compete with that expectation, certainly not a closed form like a book.

What bothered me was the degradation of my inner world. Solitude had once been something I looked forward to, the chance to curl up with a book. Now I was twitchy and irritable, constantly checking my phone. Reading at its best meant escaping an opportunistic culture and allowing one consciousness to meet another with attention and respect.

Learning to read again has therefore involved cheap tricks: motivated reading, notes, products to produce after finishing a book, notifications turned off, airplane mode, timers. It is a work in progress. Yet the habit is worth preserving, because deep reading gives us concentration, empathy, and a continuity with the world that came before the infinite scroll.
