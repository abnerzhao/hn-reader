## easy

AI was supposed to save time, but many workers now feel busier. When one task becomes easier, people often take on more tasks instead of working less.

Job boundaries can disappear. Product managers may write code, researchers may do engineering work, and engineers may need to review AI-made work from other teams. One person’s automation can create more work for another person.

AI also makes work easier to start and harder to stop. A quick prompt after work can become hours of extra work. Workers may switch between many tasks because AI makes everything seem possible at once.

Companies need clear rules for AI use, real breaks, and time for human connection. Without them, AI can make work faster while making people more tired.

## medium

AI tools do not always reduce work. Research at a tech company found that productivity gains often turned into workload creep: AI made new tasks feel possible, so people accepted more work instead of gaining more free time.

Roles expanded. Product managers began writing code and researchers took on engineering tasks. Engineers then had to review, correct, and coach colleagues using AI-generated code. Automation shifted work across teams instead of simply removing it.

AI also blurred the line between work and free time. Its conversational interface made a final prompt feel easy to send, even after someone had left their desk. Those quick requests accumulated, reducing the pauses that once separated work from life.

Multitasking increased as workers managed several AI-assisted workstreams at once. The result was constant attention-switching and longer lists of unfinished tasks. Many participants felt more productive but not less busy.

The cycle reinforces itself: AI makes work easier, so people do more of it, then rely on AI to handle the larger workload. Over time this can create cognitive fatigue, weaker decisions, and burnout.

Researchers argue that companies need an intentional AI practice: pauses before major decisions, work sequences that reduce context-switching, and protected time for human connection. Without these practices, AI-assisted work tends to intensify rather than contract.

## hard

AI tools do not reduce work. They intensify it. Research based on eight months inside a 200-person tech company found patterns of work intensification that quietly became what researchers call workload creep.

First came task expansion. Product managers began writing code. Researchers took on engineering work. Roles that once had clear boundaries blurred as workers handled jobs that previously belonged elsewhere. AI made that shift feel feasible.

One engineer described the result simply: people expected that being more productive with AI would let them work less. Instead, they worked the same amount or even more. Engineers also found themselves reviewing, correcting, and coaching colleagues who were vibe-coding. The person who automated part of a job created more work for someone else.

Second came blurred boundaries. AI’s conversational interface made starting work feel effortless. There was no blank-page paralysis and no intimidating learning curve. Workers sent quick final prompts before leaving their desks and let AI handle chores while they stepped away.

Many used AI prompts during their free time. Work done outside working hours accumulated into hours and days, with fewer natural pauses. AI made it easier to continue working when work should have ended.

Third came a surge in multitasking. Employees were expected to manage multiple workstreams at once because AI gave the impression that tasks could run in the background. The promised productivity gains became constant attention-switching and longer task lists.

The cycle is self-reinforcing: AI makes things easier, so workers do more of those things, then rely more on AI to make the larger workload easier. Participants often felt more productive, but did not feel less busy. Some felt busier than before.

Burnout is already widespread. A survey of corporate professionals found overwhelming workloads and excessive hours among the main causes. The effects were not equal across seniority, with associates and entry-level workers reporting burnout more often than senior leaders.

Companies need an intentional AI practice. That means structured pauses before major decisions, sequencing work to reduce context-switching, and protecting time for real human connection. Without such practices, AI-assisted work tends toward intensification, burnout, weaker decision quality, and lower long-term sustainability.
