## easy

People often say, “AI is just a tool. It matters how you use it.” I think this is too simple. Tools change the world around us. They affect the environment, laws, cities, work, and the way we live.

AI does not only help us do tasks. Its design can also change us. It can encourage us to depend on it, stop thinking carefully, and avoid difficult work.

Not every struggle is bad. A curb is a barrier that should be removed. But climbing a mountain, learning an instrument, writing a hard email, or lifting weights can give our lives meaning. We should remove barriers, not remove every meaningful challenge.

We need to question how AI is built, who it harms, and what it asks us to become. Tools should help us live with more dignity and meaning, not make us passive or dependent.

## medium

“AI is just a tool. It matters how you use it” sounds reasonable, but it hides too much. Cars are tools, yet they shape cities, damage the climate, and require laws, infrastructure, and safety systems. A tool is never only about one person’s use.

The same is true for AI. It matters how it is made, whose work and data created it, how it is sold, what resources it consumes, and how it changes economies and environments. Individual good intentions cannot solve every problem created by a technological system.

Tools also shape us through their design. A chair tells us to sit in a particular way. AI can encourage us to rely on prompts instead of critical thought, imagination, or the difficult work of making something ourselves.

Automation is valuable when it removes drudgery and barriers. We should cut curbs because they block access. But we should not flatten every mountain. Some effort, pain, and practice are part of learning, art, love, and a meaningful life.

AI can remove the work of writing, thinking, creating, and validating. But if it removes every struggle, it may also remove the experiences that shape us. We need to decide which burdens should disappear and which challenges are worth keeping.

This does not mean accepting tools as they are or refusing every technology. It means questioning their environmental, economic, and human effects, then reshaping them with policy, justice, and care for what makes life meaningful.

## hard

I’ve been thinking constantly about the common phrase, “AI is just a tool - it matters how you use it.” It is deceptively simple and deceptively misleading. Yes, AI is a tool. And yes, it matters how we choose to use tools. But that is not the whole story.

Consider cars. A car is a tool, but oil and gas are destroying the climate, seatbelts save lives whether or not someone is a good driver, and cars have helped make American cities unwalkable and unlivable. There is much more to tools than how we use them.

Tools are massively impactful on our environment, law, policy, and what it means to be human. Believing that AI is “just a tool” is naive at best and dismissive at worst. Nothing about tools is “just” anything. They are complex parts of life and culture.

“It matters how you use it” is also misleading. The ethics of modern technological ecosystems cannot rest solely on how one person chooses to use something. Some ethical issues are systemic and require more than an individual choosing the right method.

Even a hammer requires trees to be cut down and earth to be mined. It requires laws about workers, sustainability, sale, and use. With AI, it matters how it is made, how it is disseminated, the waste it produces, the damage it causes to economies and environments, and its overall impact on human life and culture.

Tools use us by their design. This is Heidegger’s idea of en-framing: technologies shape who we are because of their design and use. A hammer is not just wood and iron. It is a hammer because of what it does and who we become when we use it. Tools are not neutral.

We must interrogate and reshape our technologies. We need to resist design that flattens our humanity for efficiency and productivity. We need to question how tools create infrastructures and landscapes hostile to human existence.

A chair tells us to sit still, face forward, and behave. Our being is intended through the tool. AI works in the same way. We may believe it is all about how we use it, yet our tools are using us. AI can intend us to become reliant on tooling, to stop thinking critically, to stop imagining, and to avoid struggle and pain.

Automation is often sold as a way to remove drudgery. But not all struggle is drudgery. If it were possible, should we climb a mountain or flatten it? And should we climb a curb or cut it? Climbing a mountain can be the point. A curb is a barrier to access and should be removed.

At the gym, lifting weights can be rewarding and uplifting. It would be nonsense to ask a robot to lift weights for us. Yet safer machines, mirrors, cables, and protections can improve the way we lift. Some technologies enhance our human love of struggle instead of replacing it.

AI can let us avoid writing a meaningful birthday email, making a case for promotion, thinking through an algorithm, or improving an artistic craft. We can prompt and receive an answer. But automating every struggle makes it harder to tell the difference between pain that takes and struggle that makes.

Tools are powerful extensions of human will. They can include and exclude, define what matters, and shape the climate and environments we live in. We need to fight for our humanity and the health of the world, refuse unjust systems, recognize those who taught us, and keep the kind of pain that gives us shape and meaning.
