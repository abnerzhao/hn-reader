## easy

I stopped calling myself a content creator. The phrase makes creative work sound like a product in a factory.

I have a blog, readers, and a book. Those words feel more human than traffic, leads, and information products.

Words change how I see people. Readers choose to spend their limited time with my work. They are not numbers to collect.

I would rather choose my own words for what I make.

## medium

Calling a painting, a film, or a book "content" feels strange to me. The word is useful for the internet business, but it can make creative work sound like a commodity.

For years, I used that language too. After leaving my job, I described my projects as content businesses. At an independent creators conference, I heard phrases like "capturing value" and "lifetime value." They made me question the words I had accepted.

Now I call my website a blog and the people who visit it readers. My book is a book, not an information product. I do not want to call myself a content creator.

Words shape what we notice. Traffic can sound like something caught on flypaper. Readers are people who give us a piece of their day. I want language that keeps that difference visible.

## hard

I stopped saying that I create content because the phrase changes how I think about creative work. Calling Michelangelo's David "content" would sound absurd, yet the same word is now used for books, essays, films, comedy, and nearly everything published online.

The language grew with the commercial internet. "Content is king" became a slogan for an economy that needed material to fill websites, attract attention, and generate revenue. Over time, the word became a catchall label, and many writers and artists began calling themselves content creators.

I used to do the same. When I left my job in 2018, I described my projects as content businesses. Later, at a conference for independent creators, I heard business language everywhere: capturing value, distribution, and lifetime value. It made me reconsider what those terms were doing to my view of the work.

Today, I have a blog, not a content site. The people who come to it are readers, not traffic. I wrote a book, not an information product. These are small choices, but they matter because language directs attention.

Traffic feels impersonal. Readers are real people who decide to spend some of their limited time with what I make. I do not want to reduce them to leads or value over a lifetime.

The words available to us are often chosen by someone else. We can still choose our own. I may be a blogger, a writer, or an artist, but I do not need to be a content creator.
