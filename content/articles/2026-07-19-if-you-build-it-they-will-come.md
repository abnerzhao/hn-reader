## easy

When I want to join a new group, I organize something. I might plan a dinner, a reading group, or another activity that the group already enjoys.

Most communities have more people who want events than people who create them. If you make an event and invite people, they usually come. It is an easy way to meet people and become part of the group.

Events do not appear by themselves. Someone has to choose a time, invite people, and do the work. The people who do this work often become leaders in the community.

Many people consume social life without helping to create it. We can improve our own communities by doing a small amount of organizing and giving people something to join.

## medium

Whenever I have wanted to enter a new social group, I have found the fastest path is to organize events around the group’s core activity. This works whether I am new in a city, interested in a community, or simply hoping to broaden my circle.

There is usually far more demand for social events than supply. People want parties, dinners, conferences, and reading groups, but few people want to do the planning. Inviting people to an event can be as easy as giving away ice cream at the beach.

You can make friends by consistently attending other people’s events. But it is faster to organize your own event, invite the people you want to know, or help someone else with their plans. Other organizers notice who is willing to carry part of the burden.

I once assumed a social scene simply existed on its own. I thought it naturally produced activities the way a bush produces berries. In reality, events happen because someone does the legwork, and most people do not do it.

The people who put in that work often become a community’s leaders. Organizing is underappreciated by many participants, but it is one of the clearest ways to become useful and connected.

Part of social alienation may come from too many people consuming social fabric without producing it. I do not know how to solve that problem for society as a whole, but we can solve a piece of it in our own communities by creating the events we want to attend.

## hard

Several times in my life I’ve tried to break in with a new social group. I ran into a cool community I wanted to join, moved to a new city and wanted to make friends, or simply wanted to broaden my horizons.

Pretty quickly I learned that the best and fastest way to join a group is to organize events where we do the group’s core activity. In every group I’ve encountered, there is far more demand for social events and things to do than there is supply.

Getting people to come is like giving away ice cream at the beach. You can make friends by showing up from the outside and consistently attending other people’s events, but it is much easier and faster if you also organize your own things and invite the people you hope to get closer to, or help with someone else’s events.

I’ve found this true everywhere, from niche online fanfiction discussion groups to intellectual movements directing hundreds of millions of dollars annually.

Lots of people have a consumer attitude toward their communities, where they take everything for granted. I saw things this way when I was young. A social scene seemed like an automatic feature of the world, a wild blueberry bush that naturally sprouts parties, dinners, conferences, and reading groups.

Surprisingly, things do not work that way. Events happen when someone puts in the legwork to organize them. One reliable law of the universe is that if something takes a little work, most people will not do it. A scene’s leaders are mostly the people who actually bother to do the work.

Organizing is underappreciated by many people. But other organizers are very attuned to it, and they absolutely notice who else is picking up the burdens.

I have come to believe that part of today’s social alienation is a problem of too many free riders. Lots of people want to consume social fabric, but our social scripts telling people to produce it have largely fallen away. I do not know how to solve this undersupply at the scale of society, but you can solve it in your own community by supplying it.
